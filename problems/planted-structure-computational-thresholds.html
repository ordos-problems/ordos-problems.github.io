Short Statement

Can the conjectured computational thresholds for planted dense subgraph and submatrix problems be made sharp under clean average-case assumptions?

Open Problem

Give sharp polynomial-time thresholds, or matching conditional lower bounds, for planted dense subgraph and submatrix localization across the sparse and growing-structure regimes where information-theoretic recovery is possible but no efficient algorithm is known.

Statement & Model

A planted-structure problem starts with a random background object, such as an Erdos-Renyi graph or a noisy matrix, and then plants a structured signal, such as a denser vertex subset, a community, or an elevated submatrix. The information-theoretic threshold asks when the planted object can be detected or localized by any estimator, while the computational threshold asks when this can be done in polynomial time. The open problem is to turn the broad statistical-computational gap picture into sharp theorems for canonical planted dense subgraph and submatrix models: identify the exact efficient boundary, prove algorithms up to that boundary, and prove lower bounds beyond it under transparent assumptions such as planted clique hardness.

What Is Known

Wu and Xu survey a general recipe for information-theoretic limits and planted-clique reductions, and earlier work by Hajek, Wu, and Xu gives computational lower bounds for community detection in random graphs. These results explain why an intermediate regime can exist. What remains open is the sharp boundary: in many planted dense subgraph and submatrix regimes the information-theoretic limit, best known efficient algorithm, and conditional lower bound still do not coincide.

Remaining open problem. Give sharp polynomial-time thresholds, or matching conditional lower bounds, for planted dense subgraph and submatrix localization across the sparse and growing-structure regimes where information-theoretic recovery is possible but no efficient algorithm is known.
