Limited-switch contextual bandits with knapsacks

Short Statement

What are the optimal regret rates for contextual bandits with knapsack constraints when the policy is allowed only a limited number of switches?

Open Problem

Characterize the minimax regret of contextual bandits with knapsacks under a switching budget, and design efficient algorithms whose regret has the correct dependence on horizon, resource budgets, number of resources, policy complexity, and number of allowed switches.

Statement & Model

Bandits with knapsacks model online decisions that earn stochastic rewards while consuming limited resources. Contextual versions allow the reward and consumption distributions to depend on observed covariates and compete with a class of context-dependent policies. Limited-switch models add a practical constraint: the decision rule can be changed only a bounded number of times. Simchi-Levi, Xu, and Zhao solve a noncontextual limited-switch version for blind network revenue management and extend it to bandits with knapsacks. The open problem is to combine the two directions: obtain a sharp regret theory for contextual bandits with knapsacks under switching constraints, including whether the piecewise-constant dependence on the switch budget survives once policy classes and contexts enter the model.

What Is Known

Simchi-Levi, Xu, and Zhao give matching upper and lower bounds for blind network revenue management and bandits with knapsacks under limited switches, showing that regret is governed by a piecewise-constant function of the switching budget and number of resources. Earlier work gives efficient algorithms for contextual bandits with knapsacks without limited switching. The remaining open part is the simultaneous contextual, resource-constrained, limited-switch model.

Remaining open problem. Characterize the minimax regret of contextual bandits with knapsacks under a switching budget, and design efficient algorithms whose regret has the correct dependence on horizon, resource budgets, number of resources, policy complexity, and number of allowed switches.
