Short Statement

In hospitals/residents with couples (who submit joint preferences over pairs of positions), a stable matching can fail to exist and deciding existence is NP-complete. What is the precise rate at which the number of couples can grow with market size while a stable matching still exists w.h.p.?

Open Problem

Determine the tight scaling threshold (and constants) for the number of couples, as a function of market size nnn, separating the regime where a stable matching exists w.h.p.

Statement & Model

In the Hospitals/Residents problem with Couples, couples rank ordered pairs of hospitals (joint preferences over pairs of positions), introducing complementarities absent from the classical problem. Unlike the classic stable-matching problem, a stable matching need not exist (Roth 1984), and deciding existence is NP-complete (Ronn 1990). Yet in large real markets such as the medical residency match, stable outcomes are almost always found. Consider random large markets of size nnn: it is known that if the number of couples is o(n)o(\sqrt{n})o(n​) a stable matching exists with high probability, whereas if the number of couples grows linearly in nnn (i.e., Θ(n)\Theta(n)Θ(n)), with constant probability no stable matching exists. Determine the tight scaling threshold (and constants) for the number of couples, as a function of market size nnn, separating the regime where a stable matching exists w.h.p. from the regime where none exists with constant probability; and identify the sharp preference-domain conditions.

What Is Known

The qualitative landscape is well established: stable matchings can fail to exist with couples, and deciding existence is NP-complete. In large random markets, stable matchings exist with high probability under sufficiently sparse couples, including the o(n)o(\sqrt n)o(n​) regime, while with a linear number of couples nonexistence occurs with constant probability. The open problem is to identify the sharp threshold between these regimes, including constants and the precise preference-domain assumptions under which stability is likely.
